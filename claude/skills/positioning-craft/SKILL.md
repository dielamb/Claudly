# Positioning Craft

YOU MUST complete all five components. Incomplete positioning is not positioning — it's guessing.

> "When you say 'we have no competitors,' customers hear 'there's no demand for what you do.'" — April Dunford

## Step 1 — Target Customer (MANDATORY: be specific)

YOU MUST identify who benefits MOST — not who could theoretically benefit.

Answer:
- What role/context? (not "businesses" — "early-stage SaaS founders managing their first design system")
- What pain do they feel acutely RIGHT NOW?
- What outcome do they desperately want?

If the answer is "everyone" → this is WRONG. Narrow until it's specific.

## Step 2 — Market Category (MANDATORY)

What existing category does this belong to?
- Joining an existing category = faster adoption, but you inherit comparisons
- Creating a new category = requires education budget

YOU MUST pick one and justify why. "We're uncategorizable" is not an answer.

## Step 3 — Competitive Alternatives (MANDATORY: name them)

What do customers ACTUALLY use today instead of your product?
- Direct competitors (name them)
- Indirect alternatives (spreadsheets, manual process, doing nothing)

NEVER skip this. If you don't name the alternatives, the customer will — and they'll frame it worse.

## Step 4 — Differentiation (MANDATORY: must be real and provable)

What makes your approach distinctly different?
- MUST be: real, customer-relevant, and hard to copy
- MUST be expressed as capabilities → customer outcomes (NOT feature lists)
- "We have better UX" is NOT differentiation unless you can prove it

## Step 5 — Proof (MANDATORY: one strong proof beats five weak ones)

Evidence that your differentiation claims are true:
- Specific metrics ("40% faster than X in head-to-head test")
- Named customer results
- Third-party validation

## Output: Positioning Canvas

```
Target customer: [specific who + context]
Best-fit use case: [when they need you most]
Market category: [what game you're playing]

Competitive alternatives:
- [name 1]
- [name 2]

Differentiators:
1. [capability] → [customer outcome]
2. [capability] → [customer outcome]

Proof: [strongest single evidence]

One-sentence statement:
For [target customer] who [need/pain], [Product] is a [category]
that [key differentiator]. Unlike [primary alternative], [proof point].
```

YOU MUST deliver all sections filled in. Flag any section where data is missing — do not leave it blank silently.
